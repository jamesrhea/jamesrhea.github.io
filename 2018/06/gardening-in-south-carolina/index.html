Gardening in South Carolina

Fri, Jun 1, 2018 gardening

I’ve never had a green thumb. But I attribute this primarily to a lack of know-how. So I’ve set out to learn more about growing plants in my area, and will take notes in this blog. I’m starting with the basics: climate and soil traits.

Climate

The Carolinas have high summer heat and humidity, and cool, moderate winters.

Growing seasons

  • Warm: May or June through September or October
  • Cool: October or November through April or May

Hardiness zone

The South Carolina midlands fall within the 8a plant hardiness zone, which means that temperatures fall to lows of 10-15 degrees Fahrenheit.

Frost table

  • Last probable Spring freeze: May 1
  • First probable Fall freeze: October 23
  • Probable number of frost-free days: 190

Chilling hours

Fruit trees need a minimum number of hours of temperatures below 45 degrees Fahrenheit, called chilling hours. This area has approximate 800 chilling hours annually.

Soil conditions

Soil here is loose, sandy, fast-draining, and low in organic matter. Some areas have higher concentrations of clay.

pH levels

The pH scale runs from 0 (acidic) to 14 (alkaline). Many plants thrive in neutral pH range, which is 5.5 to 7.0.

Soil in the midlands of South Carolina leans acidic (low pH), in the 5.0 to 6.5 range. Compost is usually necessary. Add lime to raise pH levels.

Fertilizer

According to Katie Elzer-Peters, the author of [Carolinas Fruit & Vegetable Gardening: Plant, Grow, and Harvest the Best Edibles, “Fertilizer, whether synthetic or organic, is almost always necessary in the Carolinas” (chapter 4).

Sources

Elzer-Peters, Katie. Carolinas Fruit & Vegetable Gardening: Plant, Grow, and Harvest the Best Edibles. Minneapolis, MN: Cool Springs Press, 2013.
